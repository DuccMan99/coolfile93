Intro

The TR-505 is the runt of the roland litter. It lacks the tunability and extra sound banks of it’s big brothers, the 626 and 727, and the individual outputs of the 707. And I’m not even going to compare it to it’s analog brethren. However, as with every PCM machine, you can bend the bejeezus out of it quite easily, and quickly turn it into a digital bit-destruction beast. The sample data chips inside the 505 will give you different bends for literally every combination of pins you connect. Stick to the address and data pins and AVOID THE POWER PINS. Most of the bends change certain drum sounds in weird ways. Some will mute certain drums, or change one drum into another. I also found the individual drum outputs. You can wire these to jacks (with the jack’s ground connected to the circuit’s ground) or wire pots to the resistors for individual volume controls. You can also add individual pitch controls, and it’s even possible to replace the drum sounds with your own sample data (but we haven’t tried this yet…)

WARNING Several people have written us, saying they’ve “perma-bent” their 505s. Remember that circuit bending is potentially dangerous to ANY piece of equipment. Please bend with care!

Bends

R82 – decrease value to get distortion on the whole mix. I used a 2.2k in parallell to R82.
IC 6,7,8 – Connect pins on these chips for crazy drum sound bends
IC 17 – Try using an LED to connect pins for different blinking patterns that respond to certain drums
IC 11 – Look for “synth tone” audio outputs here
R3 – body contact with the jumpers near R80 to distort individual drums
IC 14 – Connect pin 16 with other pins to distort individual drums
C40 – Audio input

Body Contact Touch Bends

Pasha a.k.a. uoki toki contributed a few excellent BODY CONTACT BENDS. They center around one point, R46. It must be tied in with the amp somehow, because when you touch it with one hand, you can then touch other points that seem to mix noise from the chips into the output. It works on many places on the board, and isn’t even limited to the 505 board! At the moment, I happen to have next to me my HR-16, with an RCA patchbay. This patchbay connects directly to the sound chips in the HR-16, and when I touch that patchbay, I can mix in noise from those chips into the 505 output! It’s the first time I’ve ever cross-bent anything, and it was through my own body. You’ll definitely want to investigate this bend with other devices. But of course, we take no responsibility for anything you (or your static-electricity charged tabby) might blow up in the name of science. Try using R46 as a body contact bend with pins on IC11- In particular, pins 2, 5, 7, 9, 10, 24, 23, 21 and 19. It turns the drums into synth tones. Different octaves and white noise can be mixed into the signal.

Individual Outputs

C28 – C35 – You can wire up jacks to these points for individual outputs. Decreasing the value of the resistors right near the caps (R63, etc) will lower the volume of that drum in the mix. Decreasing the value of the resistors near the transistors (R64, etc) will raise the gain of that drum to a slight crunch. Instead of doing individual outputs, I was able to wire up 100k pots in parallel to the pair of resistors for each channel. This lets you bring the volume of that drum from nothing, to normal, to crunch.

Sequencer Glitches

A Big Thanks goes out to Squidfanny for discovering this bend, to The GumiElectronic for documenting it, and to Clone2 for suggesting connecting the RAM pins to ROM pins!

IC1 is a RAM chip, and it connects directly to IC3, the CPU. Circuit bending this area can be fatal to your TR-505!!! Proceed at your own risk!! At the very least, back up any user patterns you are fond of, since it’s possible to delete and/or alter them by bending this chip.

Based on comments from Squidfanny and Gumi, it seems that connecting these pins will cause the 505′s sequencer to glitch- causing stuttering notes, swapping/muting drums, etc. Whatever random notes are generated by this process will also be output via MIDI (Remember that each drum pad can be assigned a midi note). If you use these glitches while the 505 is in record mode, the new random notes will be saved to the pattern. Part of what I THINK is happening here, is that logic and/or clock pulses are being injected into the button matrix, causing rapid-fire button presses being read by the CPU. Hmm… Looking closer, none of these glitch points connect to the button matrix. Some of them will cause LEDs to blink on 1/8, 1/4, 1/2 notes, etc. It sounds like these glitches temporarily re-set the loop length. The effects are different for different patterns.

    • Jaicen
    • Dec 12, 2006, 20:21

    If you replace C34, you’ll just get a constant sound, because that is a cap which is part the output buffer. You’d want to inject your audio signal in before the voice has its envelope applied (the voices in the ROM have no envelope to allow greater data compression). To do this, you’ll need to remove some of the jumpers or traces near R13. You can trace the pins of the IC13 to find out which one corresponds to which drum sound.
    On IC13, the pinout is as follows:
    1: CONGAS
    4: CLAP/RIM
    5: SNARE
    12: KICK
    13: HATS
    14:CYMBALS
    15: TOMS/TIMBALE

    For example, if you want to replace the hat’s sound with an external source, you need to remove the topmost of the three jumpers to the bottom right of IC13 (as viewed with the jacks at the top of the board). The source input is then fed into R39 where it gets shaped by the envelope generators.
    While we’re on the subject, the envelope generators are all controlled by the row of caps to ground C20-27. If you add large caps in parallel with a 1M + pot in series, it may allow for longer delay times, but that’s not something i’ve any need for. The short nature of the ROM samples onboard do not allow for longer envelopes since they cut off abruptly anyway, so a simple large pot across each cap would be sufficient to reduce the envelope times.

    IC13 and IC14 are both demultiplexers. I’m sure Jaicen could do a better job explaining it… I’m looking at the block diagram in the service manual now… It looks like digital sound data comes out of IC11, passes thru the DAC (where it converts to analog), and goes to IC13, where it is split into the 8 audio channels. From there, it goes to the sample & hold and VCAs, made up by Q6-Q13. It’s hard to tell from the diagram what data IC14 is getting, but the output of IC14 goes to the 8 ENVelope capacitors (C20-C27), and then feeds into the VCA transistors too.

    If you want to mod the decays, i would mess with C20-C27. I’ve already noted above that the resistors that connect between those caps and transistors will lower and raise the individual drum volumes. But according to Jaicen’s comment the caps set the actual decay, not the DEMUX ICs.

    Ok, you need to stop randomly going at this board before you kill it completely!
    What exactly were you doing to the area around R1?? (Actually, what were you doing there in the first place!). The RES you see on the service manual refers to the RESET pin of both IC9, and crucially also the CPU. The transistors and resistors around that area are used to slow the turn-on of the CPU, to make sure it’s initialised last. It’s entirely possible that you shorted across one of the transistors and damaged the CPU, in which case there’s virtually nothing you can do. However, your symptoms suggest that you’ve damaged the Gate array. All is not lost though; Find C55 (next to X2 near the CPU) and cut the trace below it leading into IC9, and solder a 47K resistor from that to +5V. That will stop the gate array being hard re-set, and might bring it back, but who knows!

    Hi Justin.
    Here’s a few things I’ve tried on my TR505.

    Use or publish as you wish.

    Pitch down.
    Only way I could do this was to fit a lower frequency ceramic resonator X2 and use the pitch up mod to take it higher. I couldn’t find a way to make the
    existing resonator go lower.

    Change X2 from 1.6Mhz to 1.0Mhz.
    Note 1 : Tried 500Hz, won’t oscillate (would probably have to change the capacitors around X2 to do that).

    Note 2 : With the new resonator, oscillator locks up at max pitch unless 470ohm resistor fitted in series with pitch control pot.

    Note 3 : A 1K pot won’t return to minimum pitch – I worked out that it needed as much as 2K3 ohm – I got close with a 4K7 pot with a 3K9 resistor soldered across its terminals. The change in pitch as you turn the knob isn’t as smoothly spread over the travel as I would like. Some experiment with lin or log pots probably needed to find the best solution.

    Note 4 : I found the case of the pot and one end of the pot track was nicely touch sensitive, giving some wacky pitch bends when set to pitch up. But, it depends on how close other circuits are to the pot – some capacitance effect. I got different results with this in nearly every circumstance – so milage may vary!

    Sound? With the lower pitch, which isn’t that radical (I think 800Hz would given an octave lower, so this is maybe a 5th down?), everything sounds deeper and more “masculine”, especially the toms. I think I prefer it to the stock pitching. The kick isn’t much “bigger” – I think the output circuits are cutting the low end too much. Those with direct outs might hear the lower pitch in it’s full glory.

    I may have found another touch point. The link that lies between IC14 & D10 gives a healthy rise in volume without much “buzz” when touched – the top end seems to open out too for some reason. I think this link carries the reference voltage for the stereo output amps.

    The Sound Rom.
    Cutting 1 or 2 data out pins produce useful lo-fi digital grunge – but oddly, the lowest 2, D0 – D1, seem to do very little. I would have thought 8bit would need all it’s bits to sound clean. The easiest is the link nearest the Sound Rom pins 14 and 15 – I took that out & wired to a toggle switch.

    For those thinking of hacking the sound rom – an important note! – the data and address lines are scrambled by design. That is, Data D0 in the 505 is not D0 in the rom chip. I think this is done to make the pcb track routing easier. It doesn’t matter what order you use the address and data pins so long as you address & read the data in the same order it was written in. So if you wanted to read the rom and burn a modified one, you would need to make an adapter for the rom burners chip socket that re-routes the pins the same as the 505. You can work it out by comparing the 505 service sheet pinouts with the chip manufacturers pinout.

    If I do anymore, besides things that are already documented, I’ll let you know.

    Well, the 6 oscillators in the 40106 are a type of logic gate – inverters. When you send a 1 to the input of a gate, it sends a 0 to the output, and vice versa. In this case, a 1 is positive voltage, and a 0 is ground. That’s the real purpose of a 40106.

    In order to use it as an oscillator, we’re creating a feedback loop- looping the output back into the input thru a resistor and a cap to ground. (the resistor/cap pair determine the speed that this happens) This causes the inverter output to constantly go back and forth between 1 and 0 – or in other words, to oscillate. You can use this to make LEDs blink, or listen to it as audio.

    But we should also be able to use the inverters in the 40106 for their intended purpose- turning 1′s into 0′s – inverting the logic signals from IC1 so the LEDs light up on the downbeats instead of the upbeats.

    Hi Bill. I mentioned the “output, address bus” pins on IC3 to help folks trace the connections between IC1 and IC3 and to show that perhaps it wasn’t so risky making these connections (generally speaking, one should be very cautious when working with anything that connects directly to the CPU). So, the short answer is this: Don’t worry about the IC3 pins. Stick to connecting the documented pins on IC1 and you should be safe.

    There are a number of ways to connect the IC1 pins, and you’re right, momentary switches or toggle switches would work great (I don’t think connecting a pot would work, but you could use a rotary switch, which looks kind of like a pot). I usually start by running a wire from each usable pin to it’s own banana jack, then I’ll start patching the pins together to find the combinations I like the most. After I’ve figured out what I want, I’ll wire those ‘good’ pins to a bank of SPDT or SPST toggle switches.

    Hope this helps and have fun!

    • Justin
    • Dec 23, 2009, 11:37

    The first rule of circuit bending is you don’t talk about circuit bending.. No, that’s not right… The first rule of circuit bending is never bend anything plugged into the wall. The current from a wall outlet can kill you.

    However- “wall wart” AC adaptors convert the high voltage/current to a much lower, safer level. The power that comes out of the 505′s AC adaptor is the same as you would get from batteries.

    There is a VERY SMALL chance that the AC adaptor could fail, and pass the full 120V from the wall thru to the 505. This would melt your 505, shock you, and probably start a fire. I’ve never heard of this happening to anyone in the 10+ years i’ve been circuit bending. Personally, i dont’ worry about it, but i can’t in good conscience tell YOU not to worry about it. This is the sort of thing that you need to do a bit of research into to feel safe and confident about. If you don’t know what you’re doing, then you’re taking a risk.

    Now- as to the “foot controller” idea- How many controls are you going to make accessible to your feet? If i was going to do it, i would keep the original pots where they are, and hook up switching jacks, so you can plug in a normal expression pedal to each one. But- this might not work, because i think normal expression pedals are only 10k or so, and the volume/tempo pots are probably more. Also- The volume pot is a stereo pot, so there are more connectors than on a normal pot. Well- It should be possible to de-solder the volume and tempo pots, and put them somewhere else.

    Someone got interaction between gates of a CMOS chip, I wonder if they remembered to fit the chip supply pins with a 100nF ceramic cap (as close as possible eg across pins 14 & 7 on a 14pin chip)? Also, if it’s on a daughter board, a 10uF electrolytic cap across and immediately by the supply wires. And, if the 2 power wires are more than a few inches long, twist them together.
